Standing desk converters let you turn almost any existing desk into a height-adjustable workstation without replacing the whole setup. They are an affordable, space-saving entry point into sit-standing that works especially well for people who rent, move often, or simply want to test the ergonomic waters before committing to a full desk. The trade-off is that converters take up some of your usable surface area and often have limited height range compared to a proper standing desk. When choosing a converter, focus on three things: the weight capacity (make sure it can hold your monitors and gear), the height range (most adjust from about 5 to 15 inches above the desktop), and the footprint (a converter that is too wide or deep can crowd your typing space). Gas-spring models offer the easiest adjustment, while crank or electric models provide finer control but require more effort. For a true standing desk that replaces your old desk entirely, a full-size electric model offers the most flexibility and longevity, though at a higher price and with more assembly required.

Frequently asked questions

Is a desk converter as good as a full electric desk?

Not quite. Converters are more convenient (no assembly, no new desk) but most are less stable, have less surface area at standing height, and don't allow the full range of height adjustment a motorized frame offers. They're the right answer when you can't replace your desk, not when you're building a new setup.

How much desk space does a converter take up?

Most full-size converters are 35–48 inches wide and sit in the back third of your desk. When lowered they're compact; when raised they dominate the desk surface. Make sure the base matches your desk depth.

Can a converter hold two monitors?

The better ones can, with a dual-monitor arm or wide enough platform. Check the weight capacity and surface width before assuming, smaller converters are single-monitor only.

Are spring-loaded or electric converters better?

Electric converters are smoother and hold position without counterbalance adjustment. Spring-loaded converters are cheaper and don't need power but require a tension balance between the converter weight and your equipment. Electric is worth the premium for daily use.

Can I use a desk converter if my desk surface is glass or very thin?

Check the desk's load rating first. Most glass and thin laminate desks can handle a converter's footprint weight, but add the monitor and keyboard loads before assuming. Heavier converters on fragile desks risk surface damage at the support points.
